EcoCar UrbanConcept Suspension Hub

For this project, I designed a new hub for our UrbanConcept vehicle, which achieved almost a 50% reduction in weight while maintaining structural, maintenance, and safety requirements. The design process included multiple iterations and simulations to analyze the part and create an optimized final product.

Introduction

The wheel hub has five main purposes:

  • Acts as the connection between the wheel and the suspension
  • Mount the brake rotor, wheel, and bearings
  • Resist loading
  • Allow wheels to spin freely
  • Enable tolerances for interference fits and mount bearing locating components

Issues with Old Wheel Hub

The old wheel hub had many issues:

  • Made from multiple components bolted and adhered together
  • The original design was made over the course of a month without any structural analysis or optimization
  • Weighs over 2 kg when completely assembled
  • Impossible to disassemble without destroying the actual hub
  • Had many unnecessary components, was complicated, and over designed
  • Made from a solid steel step shaft with multiple steel plates bolted to it

Goals and Constraints
  • Easy to assemble and allow for access of the wheel, rotor, bearings, and breaks
  • Must be easy to manufacture and made from common materials
  • Must house a pre-selected brake rotor and carbon rims with a three-hole lug nut pattern
  • Adequate space must be given for the brake caliper
  • Be free spinning while also being rigidly connected to the upright

Design Iterations

    Initial designs were changed multiple times to create the final product. Creating prototypes of the part along with discussions with the manufacturer assisted this iterative process.

  • The hub was changed from extruded components and tabs to a stepped part
    • The steps allowed for the wheel to be mounted
    • Additional steps gave a flush surface for the break rotor to be located and mounted to
  • A slot was added for the lock nut washer to avoid loosening
  • Added a step at the back of the shaft for easy assembly.
    • This step would locate the bearings from the upright
  • The step where the lock nut sits allows it to properly apply preload to the bearing.
Engineering Analysis
  • Two separate static simulations were performed. One for the braking moment where the threaded holes were fixed and the load was placed on the shaft.
  • Second simulation performed utilizes forces in the x,y, and z direction. The area where the bearing sits was set to fixed
  • Simulations allowed for the optimization of strength while still being lightweight
  • Factor of safety of 2 achieved
  • Fatigue analysis shows part will not fail over long term use
Final Design
  • Weights 48% less than the original
  • Is one single part
    • Removed the extra disk used to attach the carbon rims
    • Removed the unnecessary brake disk that was used to attach the rotor
  • Shaft is now hollow and aluminum instead of solid steel
  • Easy to assemble
